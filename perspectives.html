MULTIPLE PERSPECTIVES

Three voices from the war

SOLDIERS

"According to the report, at least two enlisted men were 'panic stricken' and did not put on their gas masks. ... Mustard gas would remain on hay and manure piles, and in mud that the infantry soldiers and medics needed to walk through."
— U.S. National Archives, Gas attack report, 1918
Source: U.S. National Archives

CIVILIANS

"This Club was composed of about 15 young women who sent letters and packages of socks, tooth brushes, chocolates, biscuits, Bovril and other 'comforts' to soldiers serving overseas between 1917 and 1919."
— The Soldiers' Comfort Club, Toronto, IWM archives
Source: Imperial War Museums

POLITICAL LEADERS

"Faced with the growing use of poisonous gases on the battlefield, causing terrible injuries, the ICRC appealed publicly for a ban on their use. ... On 6 February 1918 it launched an appeal against the use of poisonous gases."
— International Committee of the Red Cross (ICRC)
Source: International Committee of the Red Cross
